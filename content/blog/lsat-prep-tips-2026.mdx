The LSAT is one of the most challenging standardized tests you will ever take — but it is also one of the most learnable. Unlike the SAT or GRE, the LSAT does not test vocabulary or math. It tests a very specific set of reasoning skills that respond remarkably well to deliberate practice.

Here are seven strategies that consistently separate 170+ scorers from everyone else.

## 1. Understand the Test Structure First

Before you pick up a prep book, know what you are up against. The LSAT consists of:

- **Logical Reasoning** (2 sections, ~50 questions) — argument analysis, flaw identification, inference
- **Analytical Reasoning** (1 section, ~23 questions) — logic games with ordering, grouping, and matching
- **Reading Comprehension** (1 section, ~27 questions) — dense academic passages and argument structure

Total: approximately 100 scored questions. You have about 35 minutes per section.

The test rewards **precision over speed** at first. Build accuracy before you worry about timing.

## 2. Master Logical Reasoning Before Logic Games

Most students jump straight to logic games because they feel gameable and satisfying when solved. But Logical Reasoning accounts for **half your score**. Even a modest improvement in LR — say, going from 70% to 85% accuracy — adds more points than perfecting logic games.

Focus on mastering these LR question types in order:

1. Must Be True / Most Strongly Supported
2. Sufficient Assumption
3. Weaken
4. Strengthen
5. Flaw
6. Parallel Reasoning

Each type has a predictable structure. Learn the structure, not just the content.

## 3. Diagram Every Logic Game

No exceptions. Students who try to solve logic games in their heads plateau around -8 to -10 in the Analytical Reasoning section. Students who diagram consistently score -2 or better.

Use a **master diagram** for each game setup, then create a fresh sketch for each question that introduces a new hypothetical. Keep your notation compact and consistent — develop your own shorthand and stick to it.

Common game types you will encounter:

- **Linear ordering** (arrange elements in a sequence)
- **Grouping** (distribute elements into groups)
- **In/out** (select a subset of elements)
- **Hybrid** (combination of above)

## 4. Read Arguments, Not Just Passages

Reading Comprehension on the LSAT is not about memorizing details. It is about identifying:

- The **main point** of the passage
- The **author's attitude** toward the subject
- The **structure** of the argument (claim → evidence → counterargument)
- **Comparative passages**: how two authors agree or disagree

Practice active reading: pause after each paragraph and ask "What is the author doing here?" The answer is usually one of: stating a position, providing evidence, acknowledging a counterargument, or drawing a conclusion.

## 5. Review Every Wrong Answer — Not Just Your Mistakes

When you get a question wrong, most people review their wrong answer. High scorers also review the **right answer** to understand *why* it is correct — not just that it is.

For Logical Reasoning, the correct answer is almost always:
- A direct, logical consequence of the stimulus
- Free of new information not in the argument
- The most conservative claim that fits the evidence

If the right answer surprises you, that is information. The LSAT has a very consistent internal logic.

## 6. Use Official Prep Tests

This cannot be overstated: **practice only with official LSAC PrepTests**. Third-party questions frequently contain subtle differences in language and logic that train you for the wrong test. The LSAT has decades of released exams available — there is no need to use unofficial materials.

Aim for:
- **Weeks 1–6**: Focused skill-building (one section type at a time)
- **Weeks 7–10**: Full timed practice tests under real conditions
- **Week 11**: Light review only — no new material

## 7. Build Your Endurance

The LSAT is three hours and 30 minutes. Mental fatigue is real. In your final month of prep, do at least four full-length timed practice tests without breaks (except the scheduled break between section 3 and 4).

If you find your accuracy dropping in the fourth or fifth section, it is almost always an endurance issue — not a knowledge issue.
